Case Study

Distracted Pedestrians: Phone Walking Study

My qualitative research roots began here: walking city streets, observing behavior in context, and learning how to challenge my own assumptions.

Research Story

I conducted in-situ observation across active pedestrian corridors to understand how people navigate attention, social signaling, and personal safety while using phones in motion. This project shaped my field-research instincts: stay close to behavior before jumping to solutions.

Key Pivot

My initial framing treated phone walking as simple distraction. The data contradicted that assumption. Participants were constantly recalibrating risk by looking up at curb edges, intersections, and crowd-density changes. I reframed the model from "carelessness" to "calculated risk management," which produced better design implications and more honest analysis.

Method

  • Street-level observational passes in varied traffic conditions.
  • Contextual inquiry interviews focused on perceived risk and intent.
  • Synthesis of behavioral cues into environmental intervention opportunities.

Why this matters

This case shows curiosity, methodological discipline, and academic honesty: I can surface insights from messy real-world behavior and adapt direction when the evidence demands it.
