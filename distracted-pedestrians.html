Field Study

Distracted Pedestrians: Motivations and Consequences of Phone Walking

Collaborative field study with Erin King, Jordan McWilliams, and Sanam Firouzikermanshahi

Overview

How do people actually use their phones while walking, and why? This study set out to understand phone-walking as a real, everyday behavior shaped by social context, habit, and a genuine desire for connection, rather than a safety problem to be engineered away. Working as a three-person team, we conducted ethnographic field observations and contextual inquiry across three cities simultaneously, then synthesized our findings into a shared analysis.

The Research

We used a hybrid ethnographic approach: each researcher embedded themselves in a public pedestrian environment in their own city and observed naturally occurring behavior over multiple sessions in October 2025.

Three sites, three cities:

  • Michigan Avenue, Millennium Park, and the Chicago Riverwalk — dense commuter and tourist corridors (Sanam)
  • Stonestown Galleria in San Francisco — an indoor mall adjacent to two universities (Erin)
  • Mill City River District in Minneapolis — open-air recreational paths along the Mississippi (Jordan)

We took field notes on our phones in real time, then followed up with contextual inquiry — unstructured walking interviews with participants recruited on-site or through convenience sampling. The goal was to capture not just what people were doing, but why they made the choices they did, in the moment they made them.

Research question: What motivates people to use their phones while walking, and how does this practice impact both the individual and the people around them?

What We Found

Three themes emerged consistently across all three sites.

Solo walkers reach for their phones; group walkers pull back.
Across every location, pedestrians walking alone were far more likely to be actively using their phones than those walking with others. This wasn't just a pattern — participants named it directly. One described using her phone while walking alone because it gives her "a sense of connection." Another told us he'd be on his phone more without his partner simply because "I would not have anyone to talk to." We also watched this shift happen in real time: a young woman walking with an older man put her phone away the entire time they were together, then picked it up the moment he stepped into a restroom — and put it away again the second he returned. The phone was filling a social gap, not just passing time.

People are managing their attention, not abandoning it.
One of the most striking findings was how calculated phone-walkers actually are. Rather than being oblivious, most pedestrians were actively monitoring their risk level and adjusting accordingly. We repeatedly observed people lowering their phones at crosswalks, glancing up when another person came close, then returning to their screens once the moment passed. During contextual inquiry, one participant articulated this clearly: "Because I don't wanna get hit by a car... I try not to have it out when crossing the crosswalk, just in case a driver wasn't paying attention." Others held their phones higher — at chin level rather than chest level — so their peripheral vision still took in the path ahead. Some leaned on companions entirely, letting a partner navigate while they stayed on their screen. The distraction is real, but it's being managed, not ignored.

A lot of phone use isn't really "use" at all.
The third theme was passive or habitual phone engagement — phones out and present, but not serving any clear purpose. We watched a participant pace in front of a store display, refreshing his email. When asked why, he said, "I don't know, I just check it sometimes." Another participant explained she reached for her phone after finishing her errand because she "no longer had a clear goal" and the phone was "something to do." At the farmer's market, we noticed a wave of phones appearing the moment people exited the market space — as if the end of one activity triggered an automatic reach. The phone isn't being consulted; it's being held onto.

Design Implications

Our findings point toward a fundamental tension: smartphones are built to maximize screen engagement, but pedestrians need to move through physical space. The phone-walking behavior we observed isn't people being careless — it's people trying to stay connected in a world where digital availability has become a social expectation.

Two directions feel promising from an HCI perspective.

The first is ambient and spatial computing. If digital information could be integrated into the environment rather than concentrated on a handheld screen, the friction between attention and locomotion largely disappears. Augmented reality interfaces that deliver relevant content within a walker's field of view — rather than pulling eyes down to a device — align closely with what Mark Weiser originally envisioned for ubiquitous computing: technology so well-fitted to context that it becomes unobtrusive.

The second is smarter notification design. Several participants described modulating their phone use based on urgency — willing to set the phone down except for messages that felt time-sensitive. Phones that filter more aggressively, or that surface important alerts through haptic or audio channels rather than visual ones, could reduce the pull toward the screen without asking people to disconnect entirely.

The deeper challenge, though, is social. The fear of missing out — the anxiety that being unreachable, even briefly, has a cost — is what drives a lot of the habitual, passive phone use we observed. That's not a design problem. It's a cultural one, and it probably won't shift until the expectation of constant availability does.

Reflections

This was genuinely collaborative research in a way that most class projects aren't. We each worked independently in the field, in cities hundreds of miles apart, and then had to reconcile our observations into shared themes. That process — comparing notes, challenging each other's interpretations, agreeing on what was actually a pattern versus what was one researcher's read — made the findings feel more trustworthy. It also made them more interesting.

The thing that stuck with me most was the attentional regulation finding. Going in, I expected to find people being reckless. What I found instead was people doing quiet, constant risk assessments, phone in hand — not unlike what drivers do every time they adjust the radio. The behavior is normalized because people have genuinely adapted to it. Whether that adaptation is sufficient is a different question, but it changes how you'd design for it.
