Case Study

Distracted Pedestrians: Phone Walking Study

A multi-city field study of how people manage attention while walking with phones in hand. Findings highlight where current street behavior creates risk, social friction, and opportunities for safer mobility design.

Overview

This research examines phone walking as a routine urban behavior. Through in-situ observation and contextual inquiry, it identifies how people continuously trade off safety awareness, social signaling, and digital engagement in shared pedestrian environments.

Key Insights

Phone walking is habitual, not occasional.

Observed: Participants often used phones while walking even without urgent tasks. Why it matters: Distracted walking is now a baseline behavior in public space. Implication: Interventions should support safer habits within everyday routines, not assume rare misuse.

People self-regulate attention based on perceived risk.

Observed: Pedestrians shifted gaze and pace near intersections, crowds, and curb cuts, then returned to screens in lower-risk segments. Why it matters: Attention is dynamic and context-sensitive rather than uniformly absent. Implication: Wayfinding, crossings, and alerts should align with moments when users are most likely to re-engage with surroundings.

“At the curb I look up automatically. Mid-block, my phone takes over again.” — Pedestrian study participant

Screen engagement redistributes effort to others.

Observed: Nearby pedestrians frequently adjusted route, speed, or body position to avoid phone-focused walkers. Why it matters: Individual device use creates collective coordination costs. Implication: Safety planning should treat distracted walking as a shared-system issue, not only an individual responsibility.

Phone presence can also serve social-emotional needs.

Observed: Some participants held phones for comfort, social buffering, or to reduce perceived awkwardness in public. Why it matters: Device use is partly functional and partly affective. Implication: Behavior-change messaging should avoid blame and instead acknowledge these psychosocial drivers.

“The phone is a social shield. It gives me somewhere to place my attention when the street feels crowded.” — Pedestrian study participant

Method

Hybrid ethnography combining field observation and contextual inquiry across three urban sites: Chicago, San Francisco, and Minneapolis.
