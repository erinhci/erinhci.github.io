Case Study

A design system built for the complexity of higher ed

EduFlow is a component library and design language for university registration platforms. Built for environments where accessibility isn't optional, consistency is hard to maintain, and dozens of teams are all building at once.

What is EduFlow

University registration platforms serve students on phones between classes, advisors on tablets during office hours, and registrars managing batch workflows on desktop. All at once, all with different needs.

EduFlow gives teams a shared visual language, accessible-by-default components, and a governance model that scales across departments without falling apart. Pick it up and build faster without reinventing the wheel every time.

Design tokens

Design tokens are where the system starts. Instead of hardcoding values, tokens give decisions a name and push them everywhere automatically. Update one token and every button, badge, and header that references it updates too.

The biggest problem in large organizations isn't bad design decisions. It's different teams solving the same problem differently. Tokens make the right choice the easy choice.

Atomic design

Atoms

The smallest building blocks: buttons, inputs, icons, badges. When atoms are accessible by default, everything built on top of them inherits those qualities automatically.

Molecules

Atoms combined into single-purpose units. A search bar, a course card, a labeled input with an error state. One job each, reusable anywhere.

Organisms

Substantial enough to accomplish something meaningful, modular enough for multiple teams to share across different products. Navigation sidebars, content grids, form sections.

Templates

Layout blueprints that define structure without prescribing content. Teams start from a template and spend their energy on the actual product problem instead of rebuilding the same page layout from scratch.

AI-powered components

These components explore what registration looks like when AI is actually useful. AI serves as a layer that supports students in decision-making and helps advisors focus their efforts.

  • Course Recommendations: Surfaces suggestions based on academic history, major requirements, and peer patterns. An explainability layer tells you why each course was recommended and lets you dismiss or give feedback. AI as a helpful nudge, not a black box.
  • Dynamic Schedule Planner: Integrates with wearables for haptic reminders, estimated walk times between buildings, and conflict detection before problems come up.
  • Advisor Analytics Dashboard: Surfaces at-risk signals like a declining GPA, missed appointments, or no contact in 30 days so advisors can triage their caseload and take action without jumping between tools.

Accessibility

Accessibility in EduFlow isn't a checklist you run at the end. It's built into every layer. Tokens enforce color contrast. Components ship with keyboard navigation and ARIA support. Patterns are validated against real assistive technologies.

WCAG 2.2 AA+ is the baseline, not the ceiling. University platforms serve a genuinely diverse population and getting this right matters in ways that are easy to underestimate elsewhere.

AI patterns

The question wasn't "how do we add AI features?" It was "how do we add AI features users actually trust?" Three principles drove every decision: transparency (users always know when AI is involved), control (AI suggests, users decide), and explainability (recommendations come with reasons).

The best AI features are ones users barely notice because they just work. When they don't, the system says so and hands control back.

Governance

A design system without governance is just a Figma file that gets ignored. EduFlow defines four groups: core team, platform teams, delivery squads, and external contributors. Each has a clear lane. Changes flow from proposal to stable release through a defined process, and token updates propagate automatically to every product that consumes them.

Nothing ships without documentation. Nothing gets deprecated without 90 days notice and a migration path. That predictability is what lets teams build on the system with confidence.

About this project

EduFlow was a semester-long project for a Managing UX Teams graduate course. The brief was to design and document a design system for a university navigating digital transformation across registration, advising, and student engagement. Not just components, but a full system with foundations, governance, AI patterns, accessibility guidelines, and a contribution model.
