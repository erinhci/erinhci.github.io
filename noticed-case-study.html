Case Study

Noticed

A self-directed HCI 595 exploration focused on reducing tracking burnout for neurodivergent users through empathetic, guilt-free interaction design.

Problem

Traditional symptom trackers assume linear consistency, which can create shame spirals when users miss entries. In interviews and secondary research, I saw a recurring pattern: people stopped tracking not because they did not care, but because the product design punished real-life variability.

Project Context

This project was developed as a self-directed concept in HCI 595 to test how accessible health tooling could support people with fluctuating cognitive and physical capacity. The scope covered framing, visual language, interface concepts, and tactile artifacts.

Design Outcome

  • Modular icon system: Users can log symptoms through low-friction visual shorthand instead of dense forms.
  • Non-linear constellation model: Each entry appears as part of a pattern over time, not as a pass/fail streak.
  • Cross-format flexibility: Mobile concepts and printable templates support low-energy and offline moments.

Why this matters

This case demonstrates niche problem discovery, emotionally intelligent framing, and an executable concept system that balances accessibility, behavior change, and product feasibility.
