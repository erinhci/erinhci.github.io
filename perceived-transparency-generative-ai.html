Qualitative Study

Perceived Transparency in Generative AI

A qualitative study examining how users evaluate ChatGPT's credibility during explanatory tasks — and why citations matter less than structure, tone, and the ability to ask a follow-up question.

Note on scope: This is a small, illustrative study (n=4) using brief, low-stakes tasks and convenience sampling. The findings are not intended to be generalizable. The value is in the conceptual direction they open up — particularly around how transparency in AI is experienced through interaction rather than built into individual responses.

The Question

ChatGPT and similar large language models are non-deterministic: the same input can produce meaningfully different outputs across interactions. This creates a genuine challenge for users trying to assess whether a response is accurate, and for researchers trying to understand how trust in these systems actually forms.

Unlike a search engine, which returns links users can verify, LLMs produce synthesized responses that feel authoritative but often come without visible citations or traceable reasoning. How do users decide whether to trust them? What signals are they actually reading?

This study used ChatGPT as a case example to explore those questions — specifically looking at how perceived transparency shapes user trust during brief, task-based interactions.

4 participants, ages 25–58
~10 min per session (task + interview)
2 themes from thematic analysis

Method

Each participant completed a short contextual inquiry followed immediately by a semi-structured interview. The sessions were deliberately brief and low-stakes to reflect everyday AI use rather than high-consequence scenarios.

Contextual inquiry (~5 min) Think-aloud protocol Semi-structured interview (~5 min) Inductive thematic analysis Convenience sampling

During the contextual inquiry, participants were asked to prompt ChatGPT to explain a topic of their own choosing — something they were genuinely curious about or didn't fully understand. They were encouraged to think aloud while interacting naturally. The goal was to surface what mental models and trust signals they were actually using, not what they said they would use in the abstract.

Topics chosen by participants included: how to draw a dragon, how to construct a high-quality paper airplane, why the moon appears larger at certain times, and how to play Dungeons & Dragons. The variety mattered — it produced different response structures from the AI and exposed how users read those structural differences.

What We Found

Two themes emerged clearly from the data.

Theme 01

Users infer accuracy from presentation cues, not citations

Participants consistently used stylistic and structural features — clarity, logical sequencing, level of detail, appropriate use of technical language — as proxies for accuracy. Not one participant mentioned wanting citations as a trust signal, and the absence of citations didn't measurably reduce confidence.

This happened even when participants acknowledged they couldn't personally verify the content. The response felt credible, so it was treated as credible.

"I actually had no idea whether they would know how to draw a dragon, but they seem to be giving me fantastic advice."

— Participant asking ChatGPT for drawing guidance

"I think it's pretty accurate. I mean, it gives me details. It gives me things I hadn't even thought of."

— Same participant, after reviewing the step-by-step response

"Yeah, I trust it. I asked a pretty scientific question, and I got a pretty scientific answer."

— Participant who asked about why the moon appears larger at certain times

The phrase "it just logically seemed to make sense" appeared in similar form across multiple sessions. Coherence and sequential structure were functioning as a heuristic for truth — which works adequately for low-stakes tasks, but raises real concerns for higher-stakes use cases where confident presentation could mask inaccurate content.

Theme 02

Transparency is co-constructed through interaction, not built into responses

None of the participants began with a stable, fixed mental model of how ChatGPT worked. Instead, their sense of what the system could and couldn't do — and how much to trust it — evolved through the interaction itself. Participants used follow-up questions to probe limitations, correct misalignments, and calibrate expectations.

"It was general enough, and the game itself is popular enough that I felt confident in its accuracy because there's so much available information about it."

— D&D participant, explaining why they trusted the overview despite needing follow-ups for specifics

One participant explicitly noted that the D&D response simplified things helpfully but that additional detail would require further prompting — and described already planning which follow-up questions to ask. Another said they would try the paper airplane instructions first and return to ChatGPT if the results weren't satisfactory. Both were treating the conversation as an ongoing process of verification, not a single-point lookup.

Tone was also a factor in trust calibration. One participant critiqued the "chaos gremlins" framing in ChatGPT's D&D explanation as distracting and tonally off for an instructional context — a signal that users are assessing reliability based on how things are said, not just what is said.

"I didn't expect the big info dump... I had hoped for a more straightforward answer."

— Participant who asked about the moon, noting that response structure didn't match expectations

Design Implications

The findings suggest that transparency in AI explanatory contexts is experienced through clarity and interactivity — not through explicit disclosure of model reasoning. Three directions follow from this:

Structure over disclosure

Prioritize clear organization, logical sequencing, and calibrated detail. Step-by-step formats with visible hierarchy consistently drove trust more than any other signal.

Support conversational probing

Users already rely on follow-up questions to refine understanding. Suggested follow-up prompts, expandable detail layers, or scope indicators would help users probe more effectively.

Align tone to context

Stylistic choices significantly influenced perceived reliability. Humor and flair that reduce overwhelm in casual contexts become credibility liabilities in instructional or explanatory tasks.

The deeper concern: When presentation quality alone drives trust, users may accept inaccurate information without flagging it — especially in low-stakes contexts where they feel no pressure to verify externally. This behavior is unlikely to be safe at scale in higher-stakes domains like health or legal contexts, where a confident, well-organized wrong answer carries real consequences. The design question isn't just how to make AI feel transparent — it's how to make it behave differently when the stakes change.

Reflection

The finding I keep returning to is how actively users were working to construct transparency for themselves — not receiving it passively from the system. The follow-up question as a trust mechanism is something I hadn't anticipated going in. It reframes how you'd think about designing for transparency: rather than building more disclosure into individual responses, you'd invest in making the conversational loop faster, more accessible, and better at surfacing what users actually need to probe.

What this study can't tell you is whether these patterns hold at scale, with higher-stakes tasks, or with more diverse populations. The conceptual direction is worth pursuing with a properly powered study. The specific numbers here are illustrative, not conclusive.
