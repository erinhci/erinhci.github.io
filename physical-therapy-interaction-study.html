Physical Therapy Interaction Study

Measuring Team Efficiency

A systems-thinking study focused on communication flow, cognitive load, and operational bottlenecks in a physical service environment.

System Overview

This project intentionally moved beyond digital screens. The core UX challenge was a human-service system: therapists, assistants, and front-desk staff coordinating under time pressure while patient needs changed minute-to-minute.

Analytical Framework

I used the Rasmussen Human Data Processor model to map perception, interpretation, and response points across handoffs, and used Occupational Stress Index indicators to tag where interruptions and ambiguity increased cognitive strain.

Communication Mapping Findings

Verbal exchanges, call interruptions, and patient-status updates were treated as "data bits" moving through the clinic network. This made it possible to identify bottlenecks where critical information was delayed, duplicated, or dropped between roles.

  • Bottleneck pattern: Phone calls regularly interrupted active care coordination.
  • System implication: Informal communication channels increased rework and response latency.
  • Opportunity: Structured handoff cues and routing protocols improved reliability.

Why this matters

This work demonstrates strategic systems thinking: I can diagnose and optimize complex service environments where UX outcomes depend on operations, communication architecture, and human factors.
