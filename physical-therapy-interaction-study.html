Physical Therapy Interaction Study

Measuring Team Efficiency

A Cognitive Engineering Approach.

The Objective

This study evaluated how communication among physical therapists, assistants, and receptionists impacts throughput, care continuity, and clinic workflow reliability across high-traffic treatment periods.

Project Impact

The framework translated interaction friction into measurable workflow outcomes and produced reusable metrics for service-level improvement in high-demand healthcare operations.

Methodology

Contextual Inquiry

Shadowed live interactions to map real-time collaboration decisions and environmental constraints.

Event Logging

Captured workflow disruptions, handoff timing, and coordination latency during clinic peaks.

Thematic Analysis

Synthesized recurring communication patterns using OSI markers and Rasmussen’s Human Data Processor model.

Key Interactions

  • Direct Verbal Communication: Rapid in-clinic updates between therapists and assistants.
  • Phone Coordination: Incoming calls for scheduling changes and treatment confirmations.
  • Scheduling System Usage: Shared software touchpoints for room assignment and appointment flow.
  • Space Management: Real-time physical navigation of treatment rooms, equipment, and handoffs.

Insights & Findings

Communication efficiency and “Vibe” emerged as critical metrics for day-to-day performance. Teams reported that ambient coordination quality directly affected focus and patient confidence, while phone-based scheduling frequently interrupted active patient care and fragmented treatment continuity.

Conclusions

The resulting measurement model extends beyond physical therapy clinics and generalizes to other expert-level healthcare environments where multidisciplinary teams must coordinate under time pressure, shared resource constraints, and frequent context switching.
